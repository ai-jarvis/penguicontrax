Q. What does Tuxtrax do?

A.

You can use this site to suggest events to happen at Penguicon, a convention of science fiction, open source software, and all nerd interests.

Tuxtrax makes it easy for staff members to delegate and help each other follow up with you, because it tracks how long your suggestion has gone without a follow-up. It also shows which events are approved and declined.

You can indicate up to five events that you "Wouldattend", and the site will try to avoid scheduling them at the same time! (No guarantees!)

(Also see Why did Penguicon make its own scheduling software?)

Q. I don't like making new accounts on websites. Can I just use my existing accounts from other sites?

A.

Yes. At the top of the page, you can log in with your existing Google, Facebook, Twitter, or Yahoo account.

Q. Each event has a button that looks like a thumbs-up. What's it for?

A.

If an event is important to you, log in and click its button to indicate this is an event you Wouldattend. Our scheduling system will see all the events you Wouldattend, and will try (as much as possible) to avoid scheduling them at the same time.

If you're logged in, at the top of the main page you'll see this:

5 "Would-attends" left

The number will decrease each time you Wouldattend something, until you can't Wouldattend any more.

Also see Why do I only get to spend 5 Wouldattends?

Q. Each listing has a blue badge with a number. 0 What does it mean?

A.

That's how many users indicated this is an event they Wouldattend.

Q. Some listings have a grey badge with a number, and a clock symbol. 1 What does it mean?

A.

That's the number of days since the event was added to the site. It goes away when a staff member indicates they have followed up with the person who suggested this event, and with the suggested program participants. It turns orange 15 if this takes more than two weeks.

Q. Is my event suggestion approved yet?

A.

At the top of the list on the main page is a legend that shows color codes. When an event has a background color, that indicates whether that event awaiting followup, followed up, accepted, or rejected. You'll get an email from Penguicon when this happens.

Q. Can I see events that were declined?

A.

Yes. Here's the page for it. We regret that for various reasons, we can’t use those event suggestions this year. Maybe next year! Also, some of them may be on that page in error, in which case please contact us, and bear with us while we fix it.

Q. How many events did I submit? I don't remember. There were a lot. I might have overbooked myself.

A.

When you log in, click on your own name to see your user page. It will list of all events you are attached to. You can also get there by typing your username after the end of this site's URL, in your web browser.

You can also search for your username in the search box above the listing on the main page.

Q. Am I scheduled to run an event too early or late in the day? Is my itinerary spread out so I have time to rest and eat? Do my events qualify me for a reduced registration rate or other rewards?

A.

This website does not yet track or show that information. Until it does, please continue to contact the head of your track, the person who contacted you from Penguicon, or the head of programming.

Q. Why do I only get to spend 5 Wouldattends? 5 "Would-attends" left

A.

Also see Each event has a button that looks like a thumbs-up. What's it for?

We limit Wouldattends because they influence our schedule. Our algorithm tries as hard as it can to prevent the events you Wouldattend from being in the same timeslot. Currently, it takes days or weeks for a computer to run the process. A person who Wouldattend everything is no different from having no preferences at all-- and would burden the system.

Eventually you'll be able to make an itinerary, and that will be unlimited. In your itinerary, you can even pretend you have a time turner and clone yourself in multiple places at once. But that feature will only be available after everything is put into a time slot.

So why shall Wouldattends be four, neither shall it be six? (And seven is right out?)

There are only forty-eight timeslots from 4PM Friday until 4PM Sunday. A human being who needs sleep might attend sixteen-- that's four on Friday, eight on Saturday, and four on Sunday, and then when would you eat? Realistically, a marathon Penguicon attendee (such as yourself) will attend ten events. The majority will attend eight or fewer.

There will probably only be five which you will remember for years to come.

For most events, if they overlap and you have to miss one, that's no big deal. However, there are some really special events which, if I miss one because they're scheduled at the same time, I would gnash my teeth, rend my raiments, and cry unto the heavens. The five Wouldattends represent those true gems. Our algorithm should only try to protect those for you.

Version 2.0 of the site will allow anyone to work for Penguicon by proofreading. If those proofreads are accepted by staff users, the system will award the proofreader with more Wouldattends! The Conchair would also have power to reward anyone with Wouldattends for doing something special for the convention.

Q. Why did Penguicon make its own scheduling software?

A.

It's difficult to find scheduling systems that support our needs. Penguicon's schedule is managed by multiple staff, because it's multiple conventions in one. Our suggestion form is different if you're running a board game, performing a concert, or leading a workshop. We also needed to integrate our event suggestion system with the system that algorithmically turns events into a schedule free of conflicts.

For years, Penguicon planned the schedule using two systems, and kept them in sync manually. In a spreadsheet, we represented rooms as columns, and times as rows, so we could catch conflicts. But it didn't store the event description, the topics, whether or not it is "just an idea"/"probably happening"/"confirmed", and other important data. We used a database to store and present a schedule with all the details associated with each event. But the databases we have used could not catch conflicts in which two events are scheduled in the same room at the same time, or a presenter in two places at one time, or duplicated events or presenters. Tuxtrax stores all the data we use, and also alerts us to conflicts, instead of duplicating the data in two systems.

It takes a lot of work to format a schedule into a printable schedule book. All too often, no proofreading is done until we see a PDF of the book. That means the desktop publishing person receives a corrected version of the schedule (or worse, receives a list of individual edits and has to edit them by hand) and has to re-do a lot of work. During the months before the publishing deadline, the proofreading should be done online. Eventually Tuxtrax will allow this. The public will be able to see the work in progress and suggest edits. The Penguicon staff will be able to click "approve" to accept each edit.

It's great to show our work to the public while it happens, to maintain excitement throughout the year.
